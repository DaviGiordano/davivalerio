Start of paper review

Summary

  • Motivation: Accurate uncertainty quantification in LLMs is crucial for trustworthy deployments.
  • Research Gap: Existing UQ methods are benchmarked against datasets without aleatoric uncertainty (AU). (Devic et al., 2025)
  • Contributions:
  • Theory
  • They show why current UQ methods work in absence of AU
  • They show why current UQ methods break down in the presence of AU
  • Empirical
  • They produce MAQA* and AmbigQA* (datasets with unresolvable ambiguity)
  • They empirically validate their theoretical claims

Aren’t there ambiguity benchmarks?

  • There are ambiguity benchmarks (E.g. AmbigSci-QA, MAQA, etc). However, these are focused on resolving the ambiguity and providing clarifications, while Tomov2025 measures uncertainty under unresolvable ambiguity.
  • Furthermore, some work uses the variation of clarifications to measure aleatoric uncertainty.

Dataset details

  • The datasets are adaptations of previous QA datasets with ambiguity.
  • The questions have multiple correct answers. They modify the questions to ask for a single answer. Therefore, its ambiguity cannot be resolved.
  • They compute the ground-truth probability of each answer by counting co-references in a proxy corpus (Wikipedia)

Theoretical results

  • Preliminaries: how they decompose total uncertainty

  • Theoretical result 1: Assuming aleatoric entropy is zero, a model with high predictive entropy necessarily has high epistemic uncertainty.
  • Theoretical result 2: Assuming aleatoric entropy is zero, a model with low predictive entropy has low epistemic uncertainty with high probability
  • The two results above explain why current methods of UQ measurement work. They are estimating an entropy that is (positively) correlated with epistemic uncertainty.
  • They prove this result for both consistency based (entropy) methods and ensemble based methods
  • They argue that under the presence of aleatoric uncertainty, this correlation cannot be guaranteed.

  • Theoretical result 3: It is impossible to disentangle EU from AU using just the model’s prediction.

Experiments

Obtaining the True Distribution p*

  • They use the English Wikipedia Wikimedia Enterprise as proxy for the pre-training corpus.
  • To estimate the frequency of each answer, they use co-occurrence frequency.
  • First, they extract keywords from each pair of question-answers (using an LLM and a prompt).
  • Then, they stem the keywords and search for co-ocurrences of keywords plus each candidate answer in the corpus [add prompt print]
  • They use an entailment model to verify the factual occurrence of each candidate co-occurance (Table 9)
  • They further validate the counts with different corpus and strategy variations. The verify that the distributions among different corpus and strategies are aligned (with the Jensen-Shannon divergence)

Output:

  • For each question, we have a set of plausible answers and the frequency of each one. (p* for each question)

Experimental Setup

UQ estimators

  • Consistency:
  • Semantic Entropy (SE)
  • Maximum Sentence Probability (MSP)
  • Shifting Attention to Relevance (SAR)
  • Internal representation:
  • Residual stream activation at layer l (?) for the final input token. Trains linear probes and 2-layer MLPs with squared error loss to predict Epistemic Uncertainty (EU)
  • Ensembles:
  • LLaMA + Gemma + Qwen -> estimate mutual information

Datasets

  • Zero Aleatoric Uncertainty (AU): TriviaQA
  • With AU: proposed datasets (MAQA* and AmbigQA*)

Models

  • Lamma3.1 8b; Gemma3 12B; Quen2.5 14B

Metrics

  • Measurement goal: How well the EU represents the true EU according to Equation 1.
  • Metric: concordance statistic. AUC_c.
  • Measures the probability that an estimator correctly ranks a sample with higher EU above a sample with lower true EU.
  • Can be interpreted as AUC-ROC.

Results

  • Estimators perform well in absence of aleatoric uncertainty (TriviaQA).
  • Exception: linear probes
  • Performance collapses when introducing aleatoric uncertainty (MAQA*, AmbigQA*)

Related Work

UQ for LLMs

Interesting remark: “Prior work has shown that hidden representations can encode factual correctedness, but has not examined settings with intrinsic ambiguity” (Li et al 2023; Chen et al 2024; Orgad et al 2025).

Ambiguity in QA

Here they mention MAQA (Yang et al. 2025) as the sole dataset to consider QA where ambiguity cannot be solved through clarification. They argue that their experimental approach is very different from Yang’s, since Yang generates all valid answers for a question, while in this paper they ask for only one. Notably, Yang focuses on quantifying LLM uncertainty due to data uncertainty.

Discussion

Limitations

  • Quantifies p* as factual occurrences in wikipedia.
  • Theoretical analysis applies only to consistency and ensemble based estimators (not for internal representations).
  • Frames UQ as classification problem
  • They do not provide an uncertainty estimator (of epistemic uncertainty) that is robust to aleatoric uncertainty
  • The defined task may not be useful downstream. I think this is not a natural task. Whenever we ask a question that allows multiple answers (to an LLM), we expect to get all correct answers.

Contributions

  • Provide formal understanding of relationship between UQ in LLMs and ambiguity
  • In absence of aleatoric uncertainty: derive correlation between uncertainty proxies and true epistemic uncertainty
  • In presence of aleatoric: show (theoretically and empirically) that current estimators are ineffective
  • Dataset for UQ under ambiguity
  • Also, their ground truth allows to measure the aleatoric uncertainty directly (entropy on p*)

Future work

  • They hope to see active modelling of uncertainty during model training

Below, additional notes – taken while reading.

Abstract

  • They claim that current UQ methods are benchmarked against tasks with no ambiguity.
  • This sounds like an odd claim to me. For example, Abg-SciQA directly addresses ambiguity. Later in the paper I infer that they are referring to QA datasets that mimic natural tasks.
  • To move on, let’s assume that we are interested in evaluating the uncertainty of an LLM while it performs a QA task. Note that we are not assessing it directly, as Abg-SciQA does.

  • They show that uncertainty estimators show performance degradation for ambiguous datasets.
  • Later in the paper they specify this further: they test ambiguous datasets where the ambiguity cannot be resolved through clarification or document retrieval. Specifically, they tackle questions with multiple correct answers, but solely require a single answer. E.g.: Q:“What is one element of the fire triangle?”  A: {Heat, Fuel, Oxigen}. [c]

  • They find performance deterioration in three UQ modelling paradigms:
  • Predictive distribution;
  • Internal representations;
  • Ensemble of models.

  • They propose MAQA* and AmbigQA*, “the first ambiguous QA datasets with ground truth answer distribution estimated from factual co-occurence”.
  • The answer distribution is the interesting bit. Assume we have the training dataset of an LLM. Take the “fire triangle” question and answer as an example. The ground truth answer distribution for this question is the number of times each answer-option appears as an answer to the question, in the corpus. For example, how many times “Heat” appears as an answer.
  • To compute this, they use the Wikipedia corpus as a proxy for the training data. First, they extract keywords from the questions of MAQA and AmbigQA using an LLM and Prompt 5 (Appendix). Then, they count co-occurences of the question keyword and each answer. [d]
  • There is the assumption that the ideal model’s output will mimic the ground co-occurrence distribution

  • They theoretically explain the performance degradation of UQ methods in datasets with ambiguity.
  • This bit is also interesting. They decompose total uncertainty into aleatoric and epistemic. Then, they prove that if aleatoric uncertainty is zero then:
  • High entropy implies high epistemic uncertainty
  • Low entropy implies low epistemic uncertainty (with high probability)
  • Therefore, measurements of entropy will correlate with epistemic uncertainty (as intended).
  • However, I find their argument for why UQ fails under aleatoric uncertainty weaker. It is simply the negation of the argument above. That is, it states that the measurement will no longer correlate with epistemic uncertainty.

  • Implication: the work reveals shortcoming of current UQ methods for LLMs. However, it does not propose solutions (maybe in the following papers of the author?)

  1. Introduction

  • Beautifully stated motivation (2026 preprint).
  • Epistemic uncertainty:
  • “how well the model understands the data”.
  • Aleatoric uncertainty:
  • suppose an ambiguous or underspecified question. Aleatoric uncertainty refers to the randomness of the distribution of true answers.

  • They tackle scenarios where questions have multiple correct answers, which are scenarios where ambiguity is not resolvable.
  • This contrasts with ambiguity present in underspecified questions, which can be resolved with clarification

  • They argue that many language generation tasks adhere to this condition (multiple correct outputs) – QA with multiple correct answers; summarization; MT; code generation.

  • They argue that most UQ use datasets with zero aleatoric uncertainty (Devic et al. 2025). Devic2025 does indeed argue that benchmarks for LLM UQ do not consider aleatoric uncertainty.

This sounds weird to me, in light of the UQ survey (Liu et al. 2025), as there exists work focused on ambiguity clarification, for example. But right, let’s restrict the study to unresolvable ambiguity.

  • Their research question is: “What role does aleatoric uncertainty play for uncertainty in LLMs both theoretically and empirically?”
  • (Their research question in ICLR was: “How do current UQ approaches perform under realistic conditions of ambiguity?”)

  • Three families of estimators they evaluate:
  • Consistency: methods that rely on the predictive distribution
  • Internal representations: methods that probe the internal states to infer epistemic uncertainty
  • Ensembles: Bayesian-inspired that aggregate predictions from multiple models.

  • They show that the methods fail when answers have aleatoric uncertainty.

How will they frame aleatoric uncertainty?

  • They theoretically show why these UQ quantification methods succeed in absence of aleatoric uncertainty.

Contributions:

  • MAQA* and AmbigQA*: the first ambiguous QA datasets with explicit ground-truth answer distributions. (estimated from factual co-occurrence statistics)
  • Empirical confirmation: existing QA methods perform at random in distinguishing high/low epistemic uncertainty questions when they are ambiguous.
  • Provide theoretical insights into causes for performance degradation under ambiguity

Implication:

  • They want these datasets to support model training (how?)

  1. Background

  • They start by detailing aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty:
  • Epistemic uncertainty: uncertainty of the model itself due to limited training data, model specification, or artifacts of optimization.
  • Can be reduced with sufficient data and a well-specified model.
  • Aleatoric uncertainty: uncertainty of the true data-generating process.
  • Irreducible by definition

  • They focus on QA problems (a diverse range of tasks can be framed as QA).

  • They use Semantic Equivalence (Kuhn et al. 2023) to group semantically equivalent responses in clusters. Then focus on the distribution over these clusters.
  • For example, Q:”What is the capital of France”. The answers A:”Paris” and “The capital is Paris" are in the same semantic class.
  • As a result, QA is framed as a classification problem.

4. A Novel Benchmark for Non-Zero Aleatoric Uncertainty

  • The key challenge in evaluating uncertainty in a QA scenario with multiple correct answers is the lack of ground truth annotations. The desired annotation are the probability distribution of the correct answers p*. (why is this the case?)
  • They obtain p* from factual co-ocurrence.

4.1 Approximating p*

  • Assumption: the probability of an outcome correlates with its relative frequency in the training data
  • Let x be a question and yi be a candidate answer, we wish to estimate p*(yi | x).
  • When computing p* from co-occurance, if yi appears with x more often than yj, then
    p*(yi | x)>p*(yj | x)
  • They point to Wang et al. 2025, which shows empirically that LLM output probabilities correlate with co-occurance statistics. Also, increasing co-occurence is linked to increase in performance.
